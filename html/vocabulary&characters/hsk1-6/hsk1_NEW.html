Pictographs (象形) - Pictures of Things

Pictographs are the oldest type of Chinese characters—they started as simple drawings of objects. While they've evolved over thousands of years, you can still see traces of the original pictures. These are the easiest characters to remember because they visually represent what they mean.

人
rén 名 person; people
Visual Origin: Picture of a person walking, seen from the side. The two strokes represent legs in motion.
Memory Tip: Looks like a person walking with legs spread apart. When used as a radical (亻), it appears on the left side of characters related to people and human actions.
Examples in HSK1:
中国人 (Chinese person)
三个人 (three people)
口
kǒu 名 mouth
Visual Origin: A square representing an open mouth. One of the simplest and most recognizable pictographs.
Memory Tip: Square = mouth opening. Appears in many characters related to speaking, eating, or openings: 吃 (eat), 喝 (drink), 吗 (question particle).
Found in HSK1:
口渴 (thirsty - mouth dry)
出口 (exit)
人口 (population)
水
shuǐ 名 water
Visual Origin: Picture of flowing water with splashes on the sides. When used as a radical (氵), appears as three dots on the left.
Memory Tip: Center line = main flow, side strokes = splashing drops. The radical form (氵) looks like three water drops.
Related in HSK1:
喝水 (drink water)
热水 (hot water)
月
yuè 名 month; moon
Visual Origin: Crescent moon shape. The two horizontal lines inside represent the moon's surface features visible to ancient observers.
Memory Tip: Looks like a crescent moon in a window frame. Also means "month" because ancient Chinese used lunar calendar.
Usage:
一个月 (one month)
这个月 (this month)

Ideographs (指事) - Abstract Concepts

Ideographs use simple symbols to represent abstract ideas. They're not pictures of objects, but rather visual representations of concepts like "up," "down," or numbers. These characters use position, quantity, or symbolic marks to convey meaning.

一
yī 数 one
Concept: A single horizontal line represents the number one—the simplest possible number representation.
Memory Tip: One line = one. Foundation of the Chinese counting system. Also used to mean "once" or "throughout."
Common usage:
一个人 (one person)
一点儿 (a little bit)
一起 (together)
二
èr 数 two
Concept: Two horizontal lines stacked to represent the number two.
Memory Tip: Two lines = two. Simple counting logic continues from 一.
Usage:
二十 (twenty)
十二 (twelve)
二月 (February)
三
sān 数 three
Concept: Three horizontal lines for the number three. After this, the pattern changes.
Memory Tip: Three lines = three. The last number to use simple line counting.
Examples:
三个朋友 (three friends)
星期三 (Wednesday)
三点 (3 o'clock)
上
shàng 名 up; above
Concept: A mark above a horizontal line, indicating the concept of "above" or "up."
Memory Tip: The small stroke points upward from the baseline, showing direction. Used in: 上午 (morning), 上学 (go to school).
Common uses:
上午 (morning)
上班 (go to work)
楼上 (upstairs)
下
xià 名 down; below
Concept: A mark below a horizontal line, indicating "below" or "down."
Memory Tip: The stroke hangs down from the baseline. Opposite of 上. Used in: 下午 (afternoon), 下雨 (rain falls).
Examples:
下午 (afternoon)
下雨 (rain)
下班 (get off work)
大
dà 形 big
Concept: A person (人) with arms stretched wide to show "bigness."
Memory Tip: Person with arms spread wide = taking up lots of space = big. Component in: 太 (too/very).
Usage:
大学 (university)
大家 (everyone)
很大 (very big)
小
xiǎo 形 small
Concept: Three small drops or divisions, representing something divided into small parts.
Memory Tip: Something divided (丿八) becomes smaller. Used in: 小姐 (Miss), 小学生 (elementary student).
Examples:
小狗 (small dog; puppy)
小姐 (Miss; young lady)
小时 (hour)

Compound Ideographs (会意) - Combined Meanings

Compound ideographs combine two or more meaningful parts to create a new meaning. The meaning comes from the logical relationship between the components. These are like visual equations: component A + component B = new meaning C.

好
hǎo 形 good
Components: 女 (woman) + 子 (child) = good
Memory Tip: In ancient China, a woman with a child represented family harmony = "good." One of the most famous compound ideographs.
Common uses:
好吃 (delicious)
很好 (very good)
你好 (hello)
看
kàn 动 to see; to look at
Components: 手 (hand) + 目 (eye) = to look
Memory Tip: Hand shading eyes to see better = looking carefully. The hand component appears as 扌on top.
Usage:
看书 (read a book)
看电视 (watch TV)
看见 (to see)
坐
zuò 动 to sit
Components: Two 人 (people) + 土 (earth) = to sit
Memory Tip: Two people sitting on the ground. The people face each other in conversation while seated.
Usage:
坐下 (sit down)
坐车 (take a vehicle)
请坐 (please sit)

Phonetic Compounds (形声) - Sound + Meaning

About 80% of Chinese characters are phonetic compounds. They combine a semantic component (meaning) with a phonetic component (sound hint). The semantic part tells you the general category, while the phonetic part hints at pronunciation. This is the most productive way Chinese creates new characters.

妈
mā 名 mother
Components: 女 (woman) + 马 (mǎ - horse)
Semantic: female family member | Phonetic: mǎ → mā
Memory Tip: Woman (女) + sound "ma" (马) = mama/mother. The horse provides the "ma" sound.
Usage:
妈妈 (mom)
我妈妈 (my mother)
妈妈好 (Hello, mom)
吗
ma 助 [question particle]
Components: 口 (mouth) + 马 (mǎ - horse)
Semantic: spoken particle | Phonetic: mǎ → ma
Memory Tip: Mouth (口) + "ma" sound (马) = question particle "ma". Same phonetic as 妈 but different semantic component.
Question examples:
你好吗? (How are you?)
是学生吗? (Are you a student?)
喜欢吗? (Do you like it?)
请
qǐng 动 please; to invite
Components: 讠 (speech) + 青 (qīng - green/blue)
Semantic: polite speech | Phonetic: qīng → qǐng
Memory Tip: Speech radical + "qing" sound = polite request. The speech radical shows it's about communication.
Polite uses:
请坐 (please sit)
请问 (excuse me, may I ask)
请进 (please come in)
谁
shéi 代 who?
Components: 讠 (speech) + 隹 (zhuī - short-tailed bird)
Semantic: question word | Phonetic: modified sound
Memory Tip: Speech radical indicates it's a question word. Think "Who's speaking?"
Question examples:
谁是老师? (Who is the teacher?)
你是谁? (Who are you?)
谁的书? (Whose book?)
话
huà 名 speech; language
Components: 讠 (speech) + 舌 (shé - tongue)
Semantic: speech + tongue = spoken language
Memory Tip: Speech + tongue = talking/language. Used in: 说话 (speak), 电话 (telephone).
Usage:
说话 (speak; talk)
电话 (telephone)
中国话 (Chinese language)
们
men suffix [plural marker for people]
Components: 亻 (person) + 门 (mén - door)
Semantic: people | Phonetic: mén → men
Memory Tip: Person radical + "men" sound = plural people. 我们 (we), 他们 (they).
Plural forms:
我们 (we; us)
他们 (they; them)
老师们 (teachers)

Particles & Function Words

Chinese particles and function words are grammatical tools that don't have concrete meanings but are essential for sentence structure. They mark questions, show relationships, indicate completed actions, and more. These are some of the most frequently used characters in Chinese.

的
de 助 possessive/descriptive particle
Function: Links descriptions to nouns, shows possession
Components: 白 (white) + 勺 (ladle)
Memory Tip: Most common character in Chinese! Think of it as "'s" in English or as a linking word.
Examples:
我的书 (my book)
好吃的菜 (delicious food)
中国的茶 (Chinese tea)
了
le 助 completed action/change particle
Function: Shows completed actions or changed states
Memory Tip: Think "I did it!" The hook shape suggests completion, like a checkmark.
Examples:
吃了 (ate/have eaten)
来了 (came/has come)
好了 (OK now/ready)
吗
ma 助 yes/no question particle
Function: Turns statements into yes/no questions
Memory Tip: Add "ma" to any statement to make it a question. Like adding "?" in English.
Examples:
好吗? (Is it good?)
你是学生吗? (Are you a student?)
呢
ne 助 question particle; how about...?
Function: Returns questions, asks "how about...?"
Memory Tip: Bounces questions back like a tennis ball. "And you?"
Examples:
你呢? (How about you?)
我很好，你呢? (I'm fine, and you?)
